‘She bullies and berates’: University of Florida faculty evaluations of Rosenbury allege pattern of academic freedom, faculty governance violations

By Maya Stahl. Graphics by Diya Nair. Edited by Esha Karam, Sarah Huddleston, Shea Vance, August Phillips, Yvin Shin, Ella Ahner, and Neena Dzur

August 24, 2024

Frances Cohen / Staff Photographer

Faculty members at the University of Florida Levin College of Law criticized Barnard President Laura Rosenbury’s time as law school dean over concerns related to faculty governance, academic freedom, and leadership in a faculty evaluation from the 2019-20 academic year obtained by Spectator.

The law school’s dean evaluation committee, a group of five faculty members, oversaw the report as Rosenbury’s contract came up for renewal. The report—created as an informal survey for faculty members to anonymously evaluate Rosenbury’s tenure—asked a range of questions about Rosenbury’s leadership, transparency with faculty, and success in fundraising for the school.

The Levin College of Law employed about 90 full-time faculty in 2019, and the anonymous survey received responses from 58 faculty members.

A majority of respondents said that Rosenbury had positively impacted “the institution’s image” and worked “effectively to lead the college’s fundraising efforts.” Rosenbury served as dean of the law school from 2015 to 2023, where she helped boost the school’s U.S. News & World Report law school ranking from 48 to 21. During her tenure, the school saw an over 200 percent increase in applications and a $100 million increase in donations.

Rosenbury received the lowest marks in categories regarding her relationship to and work with faculty. Of faculty who responded to the survey, almost 60 percent disagreed with the statement, “The dean’s leadership style promotes open communication with faculty,” and about 46 percent “strongly disagreed” with the statement, “The dean seeks input from faculty when making important decisions.”

In response to a question about administration and management, multiple faculty members described Rosenbury’s leadership as “autocratic.” Comments also criticized Rosenbury’s communication with faculty and the state of faculty governance during her tenure, citing a lack of transparency regarding administrative and financial decisions.

Others described Rosenbury as “a model of good leadership.”

“She is an absolute star when it comes to engaging stakeholders in the law school. She is a great ambassador for the law school with the students, the donors, and the administration,” one response reads. “She supports the faculty, and acknowledges when she is working within constraints that are beyond our power to change.”

Joe Glover, former provost of the University of Florida during Rosenbury’s tenure, told Spectator he was “always a little puzzled by the faculty reaction” to Rosenbury and criticisms of her record regarding academic freedom. “I always found Dean Rosenbury to be a staunch defender of academic freedom,” he said.

“Throughout her career, President Rosenbury has fought to protect academic freedom and create environments where students and faculty engage in open and respectful discourse,” a Barnard spokesperson wrote in a statement to Spectator. “She is committed to fostering a Barnard that is inclusive, diverse, and welcoming to all – a place where people’s engagement with different perspectives allows them to deepen their understanding of the world.”

Rosenbury recently announced the formation of a faculty committee on free expression at Barnard, which is currently taking nominations through the faculty governance and procedures committee. The committee will begin work in the fall, according to the spokesperson.

Barnard faculty passed a vote of no confidence in Rosenbury in April, the first such recorded vote in the college’s history. A week prior, the Barnard chapter of the American Association of University Professors unanimously issued a vote of no confidence in Rosenbury for her “Lack of care for students,” “Ignoring shared governance,” “Repeated violations of academic freedom and free expression,” “Administrative chaos at every level of the college,” and “Undermining the longstanding and cherished culture of Barnard College.”

In the survey, Levin College of Law faculty detailed anecdotes of Rosenbury “pounding the table, yelling, belittling, lying, and harassing” faculty and staff and described her as unwilling to collaborate with faculty members. One wrote, “the things she accomplished is through unilateral action and bullying anybody who might voice an opposing opinion.” Several faculty members compared her leadership style to former President Donald Trump.

“She’s Trump in a skirt,” Michelle Jacobs, emeritus professor at the Levin College of Law, said in an interview with Spectator.

In response to the survey question, “What do you consider the Dean’s strengths?” one individual wrote, “Leave, please, please, please leave our College. Go on your way and leave us.”

Another response reads, “The Dean does not lead, she bullies and berates.”

“If she were engaging this behavior as a practicing lawyer, she’d be subject to disbarment. It IS THAT bad,” the faculty member added.

Jacobs told Spectator that law school faculty were prohibited from placing flyers on bulletin boards and their office doors. She said her posters and information were consistently torn down or removed when she was not present.

“I had the 12 warning signs of fascism, and I put that on the bulletin board,” Jacobs said. “So the posters would be out while I was there, when I came back the next Tuesday, it had been taken down.”

“This is how we lived,” she added.

Neil Buchanan, professor emeritus of law and former director of global scholarly initiatives at the University of Florida, said that faculty organized an informal, anonymous survey through a faculty-led committee because they had no formal process or structure to provide input on Rosenbury’s leadership. Buchanan said the personnel evaluation of Rosenbury is similar to a “slam book” and was not done in “good faith.”

In 2016, the Chronicle of Higher Education published an investigation detailing Rosenbury’s “retaliatory style” of leadership. In the report, several staff members allege Rosenbury yelled at them and used profanities. In her interview with the Chronicle, she noted that profanity may be “interpreted differently in the South as opposed to other legal environments” and said “I am now much more careful about my language.”

After graduating from Harvard Law School, Rosenbury spent five years practicing law or clerking before transitioning into professorship.

When Rosenbury was first announced as the ninth president of Barnard, she was praised for her ability to fundraise, a sentiment echoed in the 2019-20 evaluation. But many University of Florida faculty said in the evaluation that there was a lack of transparency surrounding how she spent the budget. A majority of respondents disagreed with the claim that “the Dean keeps faculty informed about budgets and resource allocation.”

In the 2019-20 evaluation, faculty members criticized Kelli Murray—who served on Rosenbury’s senior administrative team at the University of Florida from 2017 to 2020—for her lack of transparency and communication regarding the school’s budget.

“What’s happening with all our donor funds? Do they know if the money is being used for the purpose it was donated? Why is no one allowed to see account balances anymore except for Kelli Murray?” one faculty member wrote. “Maybe it’s insecurity? When she does share it’s always selective, carefully packaged and slanted to make her look good and all outside constituencies receive information long before the faculty.”

Rosenbury appointed Murray as executive vice president for strategy and chief administrative officer at Barnard, announcing her appointment after Murray began working in July 2023. Since her arrival at Barnard, Murray has acquired more administrative oversight of the college, assuming the responsibilities of Sarah Gillman, Barnard’s former senior vice president for strategic finance and operations, following her departure in June, according to an email sent to faculty.

During Rosenbury’s tenure as dean of Levin College of Law, the University of Florida came under fire for conflict-of-interest policies some say infringed on its faculty’s academic freedom. The university instated policies restricting faculty members’ ability to serve as expert witnesses or sign onto amicus curiae briefs in cases deemed as conflicting with the university’s interests—including a case challenging Florida’s recently enacted voter suppression law.

A 2021 lawsuit against the University of Florida and its top administrators named Rosenbury as a defendant who was “tasked with enforcing the University’s Conflict of Interest Policy as it applies to Law School professors.” In a 2023 AAUP report titled “Political Interference and Academic Freedom in Florida’s Higher Education System,” four faculty members from the Levin College of Law “gave accounts of being barred from testifying or ordered to omit mention of their university positions in court statements.”

In an email sent to law school faculty, the lawsuit alleged, Rosenbury wrote that “Entities of the College of Law” who wish to participate in cases against the state of Florida “must separately receive approval from me, the General Counsel, and President Fuchs.”

The district court found in January 2022 that professors prohibited from providing expert testimony “are suffering the ongoing injury of self-censorship as a result of the challenged policy.” The case was dismissed after the University of Florida amended its policies to be content neutral by restricting only expert testimony that posed a potential conflict of commitment, rather than a conflict of interest.

Henry Reichman, CC ’69—professor emeritus of history at California State University, East Bay, former vice president of the AAUP, and co-chair of the AAUP Special Committee on Academic Freedom in Florida’s Public Higher Education System—said that “the level of disappointment verging on anger toward Dean Rosenbury was quite remarkable.”

“They all argued that they saw her, rather than as someone who, as a dean, should be defending the rights of her faculty and defending the principles of academic freedom, she seems to be someone going overboard to accommodate the state and the governor, when pretty much everybody in Florida higher education was sounding alarm bells,” Reichman said.

Multiple Levin College of Law faculty members expressed “disbelief” in Rosenbury’s appointment at Barnard, according to a preliminary iteration of the 2023 AAUP report. The announcement of Rosenbury’s appointment at Barnard came after the case was dismissed.

Both Reichman and Jacobs agreed that faculty, staff, and students need to organize and “speak out more boldly” against violations of academic freedom.

“With Laura, you have to speak out,” Jacobs said. “You have to fight every single inch of the way, because if you give into her even a little bit, she will push that to the next most extreme level. By the time you realize what’s happening, it’s way too late.”
